\section{\label{sec:intro}Introduction}

Network growth models are central to our understanding of complex systems in the real world. The Barab\'asi--Albert (BA) model introduced preferential attachment as a mechanism for generating power-law degree distributions, while copying models propose an alternative microscopic rule in which new nodes replicate the connections of existing ones.

In this work we analyze the \emph{all-or-none} model. At each growth step a new node is added and connected to a target chosen uniformly at random, and then it connects to \emph{all} neighbors of the target with probability $p$, or to \emph{none} of them with probability $1-p$. We show that despite its simplicity, the model produces power-law degree distributions with exponent $1/p$, an $N$-independent clustering coefficient $C=3p/(1+2p)$, and logarithmic average distances, making it a small-world network for every $p\in(0,1)$. Additionally, we will also investigate the model's assortative nature, as well as its consensus dynamics.

\section{\label{sec:model}Model Definition}

The network begins with a single node ($N=1$). At each growth step ($N\to N+1$):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A new node $v$ is introduced.
  \item A target node $u$ is chosen uniformly at random from the existing $N$ nodes.
  \item An edge $(v, u)$ is added.
  \item With probability $p$, edges $(v, w)$ are added for every neighbor $w$ of $u$; with probability $1-p$, no further edges are added.
\end{enumerate}

\section{\label{sec:edges}Number of Edges}

Let $M$ denote the expected number of edges in the network, and write $M_N$ to specify when the network has $N$ nodes. At step $N\to N+1$, a target $u$ is chosen uniformly from the $N$ existing nodes. The expected number of new edges is $1 + p\,k_u$, where $k_u$ is the degree of $u$. Note that by the handshake lemma, the average value of $k_u$ over all choices of $u$ is $2M_N/N$. It follows that,
\begin{align}\label{eq:edge_rec}
  M_{N+1} = \left(1 + \frac{2p}{N}\right)M_N + 1.
\end{align}
This is a first-order linear recurrence. A particular solution of the form $\alpha_M N$ yields $\alpha_M = 1/(1-2p)$ for $p\neq 1/2$. The homogeneous solution is $\mathcal{A}_N = \Gamma(N+2p)/[\Gamma(1+2p)\,\Gamma(N)]$, satisfying $\mathcal{A}_{N+1}/\mathcal{A}_N = (N+2p)/N$. Imposing $M_1=0$ now yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:edge_exact}
  M = \frac{1}{1-2p}\left(N - \frac{\Gamma(N+2p)}{\Gamma(1+2p)\,\Gamma(N)}\right),
\end{equation}
for $p\neq 1/2$. Since $\Gamma(N+2p)/\Gamma(N)\sim N^{2p}$ as $N \to \infty$, two regimes emerge: in the \textit{sparse} regime ($p < 1/2$), the growth $M\sim \frac{N}{1-2p}$ is linear, whereas in the \textit{dense} regime ($p > 1/2$), the growth $M\sim \frac{N^{2p}}{(2p-1)\,\Gamma(1+2p)}$ is superlinear.

\section{\label{sec:degree}Degree Distribution}

Denote by $f(k, i, N)$ the probability that node $i$ has degree $k$ when the network has $N$ nodes. At step $N\to N+1$, degree $k$ increases by one with probability $(1+pk)/N$, because either the node is chosen as target (probability $1/N$) or a neighbor of it is chosen and the ``all'' event occurs (probability $pk/N$). Thus,
\begin{align}\label{eq:master_node}
  f(k, i, N+1) 
  &= \frac{1+p(k-1)}{N}\,f(k-1, i, N) \notag\\
  &\qquad+ \left(1-\frac{1+pk}{N}\right)f(k, i, N).
\end{align}
Assume that $p < 1/2$ so that the expected number of edges $M$ is linear in $N$, by the results of Section~\ref{sec:edges}. This allows us to seek the asymptotic degree distribution $P_k = \lim_{N\to\infty} n_k(N)/N$, where $n_k(N)=\sum_{i=1}^{N}f(k,i,N)$ is the expected number of nodes with degree $k$ in the $N$-node network. Note that the new node enters with degree $1$ with probability $1 - p$ and with degree $k$ with probability $p\,n_{k-1}(N)/N$ for $k \geq 2$. Hence, summing \eqref{eq:master_node} over the $N$ existing nodes and adding the new node yields
\begin{align}
  n_k(N+1) 
  &= \frac{1+p(k-1)}{N}\,n_{k-1}(N) + \left(1-\frac{1+pk}{N}\right)n_k(N) \notag\\
  &\quad+ (1 - p)\,\delta_{k,1} + \frac{p\,n_{k-1}(N)}{N}.
\end{align}
Substituting $n_k(N) = NP_k$, we have $(2+pk)\,P_k = (1+pk)\,P_{k-1} + (1-p)\,\delta_{k,1}$. This yields $P_1 = (1-p)/(2+p)$ and, for $k\ge 2$, we have $P_k = \frac{1+pk}{2+pk}\,P_{k-1}$, which has the closed form
\begin{equation}
  P_k = \frac{1-p}{2+p}\prod_{j=2}^{k}\frac{1+pj}{2+pj}, \quad k\ge 1. \notag
\end{equation}
In terms of the Gamma function,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:degree_dist}
  P_k = \frac{(1-p)\,\Gamma(2+2/p)}{(2+p)\,\Gamma(2+ 1/p)} \cdot \frac{\Gamma(k+1+ 1/p)}{\Gamma(k+1+2/p)}.
\end{equation}
As $k \to \infty$, we have $\Gamma(k+1+ 1/p)/\Gamma(k+1+2/p)\sim k^{-1/p}$, and so $P_k \sim \lambda_p k^{-1/p}$, where $\lambda_p = (1-p)\,\Gamma(2+2/p)/[(2+p)\,\Gamma(2+1/p)]$. The degree distribution thus follows a power law with exponent $1/p$, matching the degree distribution of the BA model at $p=1/3$.

\section{\label{sec:clustering}Triangles and Clustering}

Let $T$ denote the expected number of triangles in the network, and write $T_N$ to specify when the network has $N$ nodes. Triangles are created in an all-or-none network if and only if the ``all'' event occurs and $N \geq 2$. If the target $u$ has degree $k_u$ and there are $e_u$ edges among its neighbors, the new node $v$ forms $k_u$ triangles of type $(v, u, w_i)$ and $e_u$ triangles of type $(v, w_i, w_j)$, where $w_i, w_j$ are distinct neighbors of $u$. Note that $\sum_u k_u = 2M_N$ and $\sum_u e_u = 3T_N$. Averaging over the target choice $u$ thus yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tri_rec}
  T_{N+1} = \left(1+\frac{3p}{N}\right)T_N + \frac{2p\,M_N}{N}.
\end{equation}
This linear recurrence has the same structure as~\eqref{eq:edge_rec}. Substituting the exact edge formula and solving with $T_1=0$ gives, for $p\neq 1/2, 1/3$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tri_exact}
  T = \frac{2pN}{(1-2p)(1-3p)} + \frac{2\mathcal{A}_N}{1-2p} - \frac{2\mathcal{B}_N}{1-3p},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{A}_N$ is as in~\eqref{eq:edge_exact} and $\mathcal{B}_N = \Gamma(N+3p)/[\Gamma(1+3p)\Gamma(N)]$. Thus two regimes emerge as $N \to \infty$: for $p<1/3$, the growth $T\sim \frac{2p}{(1 -2p)(1 -3p)}N$ is linear, and for $p > 1/3$, the growth $T\sim \frac{2}{\Gamma(1+3p)(3p-1)}N^{3p}$ is superlinear.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.8\columnwidth]{figures/triangles_vs_p.pdf}
  \caption{Expected number of triangles $T$ as a function of $p$ for $N=50,200,500$, averaged over $10^3$ trials. Dashed lines show the analytical prediction \eqref{eq:tri_exact}, fitting the simulation results across all tested $N$.}
  \label{fig:triangles}
\end{figure}

We now derive the global clustering coefficient $C = 3T/W$, where $W = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i} k_i(k_i-1)$ is the expected number of connected triples. Let $S_N = \sum_i k_i^2$, and note that $W=(S_N-2M_N)/2$. To derive a recurrence for $S_N$, note that when a node gains one link ($k\to k+1$), its contribution to $S$ changes by $(k+1)^2-k^2=2k+1$. At step $N\to N+1$, the target $u$ always gains degree 1, and in the ``all'' event each neighbor $w$ of $u$ also gains degree 1. On the other hand, the new node enters with degree $k_v=1$ or $1+k_u$. Summing up all contributions, the expected change in $S$ given target $u$ is
\begin{align}
  \E[\Delta S \,|\, u] = (1\!-\!p) + p(1\!+\!k_u)^2 + (2k_u\!+\!1) + p\!\sum_{w\sim u}\!(2k_w\!+\!1), \notag
\end{align}
Notice that
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sum_of_neighbors}
  \sum_u\sum_{w\sim u}k_w = \sum_i k_i^2 = S,
\end{equation}
as each node $w$ appears as a neighbor of exactly $k_w$ other nodes. Thus, averaging over the uniform choice of $u$, and using $\sum_u k_u = 2M$, $\sum_u k_u^2 = S$, and \eqref{eq:sum_of_neighbors} gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:S_rec}
  S_{N+1} = \left(1+\frac{3p}{N}\right)S_N + 2 + \frac{(4+6p)M_N}{N}.
\end{equation}
Solving~\eqref{eq:S_rec} by the same method used for~\eqref{eq:edge_rec} and~\eqref{eq:tri_rec} gives, for $p\neq 0,\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{3}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:S_exact}
  S = \frac{(6+2p)N}{(1-2p)(1-3p)} + \frac{(4+6p)\mathcal{A}_N}{p(1-2p)} - \frac{4(1+2p)\mathcal{B}_N}{p(1-3p)},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{A}_N$ and $\mathcal{B}_N$ are as in~\eqref{eq:tri_exact}. Recall that $W=(S-2M)/2$, so substituting \eqref{eq:edge_exact} and \eqref{eq:S_exact} yields 
\begin{equation}
  W = \frac{2(1 + 2p)}{p}\left[\frac{pN}{(1 -2p)(1 -3p)} + \frac{\mathcal{A}_N}{1 -2p} - \frac{\mathcal{B}_N}{1 -3p}\right], \notag
\end{equation}
Comparing with $3T$ from~\eqref{eq:tri_exact},
\begin{equation}
  3T = 6\left[\frac{pN}{(1 -2p)(1 -3p)} + \frac{\mathcal{A}_N}{1 -2p} - \frac{\mathcal{B}_N}{1 -3p}\right], \notag
\end{equation}
we see that $W$ and $3T$ have the same bracketed term, and thus we get
\begin{equation}\label{eq:clustering}
  C = \frac{3p}{1+2p},
\end{equation}
for all $p\in(0,1)$ as $N \to \infty$. In particular, this result is constant on $N$, which is large compared to the clustering coefficient of the ErdĹ‘s-RĂ©nyi random graph and thus a sign for the small-world property.

\section{\label{sec:assortativity}Assortativity}

Let $X, Y$ be the random variables representing the degrees of two nodes of an edge chosen uniformly at random from the network. The degree assortativity coefficient \cite{newman} is the Pearson correlation coefficient of the degree of pairs of adjacent nodes:
\begin{equation}
  r = \frac{\E[(X - \E[X])(Y - \E[Y])]}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y} =  \frac{\E[XY] - \E[X]\E[Y]}{\E[X^2] - (\E[X])^2}. \notag
\end{equation}
Note that $\E[X] = \E[Y] = \frac{1}{|E|}\sum_{(u, v) \in E} \frac{1}{2}(k_u + k_v)$, $\E[X^2] = \E[Y^2] = \frac{1}{|E|}\sum_{(u, v) \in E} \frac{1}{2}(k_u^2 + k_v^2)$, and $\E[XY] = \frac{1}{|E|}\sum_{(u, v) \in E} k_uk_v$. Thus we have
\begin{align} \label{eq:assortativity_formula}
 r
 &= \frac{\frac{1}{|E|}\sum_{(i, j)\in E} k_ik_j - \frac{1}{|E|^2}\left[\sum_{(i, j) \in E} \frac{1}{2}(k_i + k_j)\right]^2}{\frac{1}{|E|}\sum_{(i, j)\in E} \frac{1}{2}(k_i^2 + k_j^2) - \frac{1}{|E|^2}\left[\sum_{(i, j) \in E} \frac{1}{2}(k_i + k_j)\right]^2} \notag \\
 &= \frac{4MR - S^2}{2MQ - S^2},
\end{align}
where $M = |E|$ and $S = \sum_i k_i^2$ are defined as before, $Q = \sum_i k_i^3$, and $R=\sum_{(i,j)\in E}k_ik_j$. Let $Q_N$ and $R_N$ denote the corresponding quantities when the network has $N$ nodes. 

We first derive a recurrence for $Q_N$. Note that when a node $i$ gains one edge ($k_i\to k_i+1$), its contribution to $Q$ changes by $(k_i+1)^3-k_i^3=3k_i^2+3k_i+1$. At step $N\to N+1$, the target $u$ always gains degree~1, and in the ``all'' event each neighbor $w$ of $u$ also gains degree~1. On the other hand, the new node enters with degree $k_v=1$ or $1+k_u$. Summing up all contributions, the expected change in $Q$ given target $u$ is
\begin{align}
  \E[\Delta Q\mid u] &= (1-p)\cdot 1 + p(1+k_u)^3 + (3k_u^2+3k_u+1) \notag \\
  &\quad+ p\sum_{w\sim u}(3k_w^2+3k_w+1). \notag
\end{align}
By the same argument as~\eqref{eq:sum_of_neighbors}, $\sum_u\sum_{w\sim u}k_w^2 = \sum_w k_w^3 = Q$. Thus, averaging over the uniform choice of $u$, and using $\sum_u k_u = 2M$, $\sum_u k_u^2 = S$, $\sum_u k_u^3 = Q$, and~\eqref{eq:sum_of_neighbors} yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Q_rec}
  Q_{N+1} = \left(1+\frac{4p}{N}\right)Q_N+2+\frac{(3+6p)S_N+(6+8p)M_N}{N}. \notag
\end{equation}

To derive a recurrence for $R_N$, note that when node $i$ gains one edge, the product $k_ik_j$ on each incident edge $(i,j)$ increases by $k_j$. If both endpoints gain degree simultaneously, the product increases by $k_i+k_j+1$ instead. Write $\pi_i = \sum_{j\sim i}k_j$. We consider the two events separately.

\emph{``None'' event.} Only the target $u$ gains degree~1, and the single new edge $(v,u)$ contributes $k_v\cdot k_u^{\mathrm{new}}=k_u+1$. The total change is thus $\Delta R_{\mathrm{none}} = \pi_u + k_u + 1$.

\emph{``All'' event.} The target $u$ and all its neighbors gain degree~1, so the set of nodes whose degree increases is $\{u\}\cup N(u)$. Each node $i$ that gains degree~1 increases the product $k_ik_j$ by $k_j$ on every incident edge $(i,j)$, contributing $\pi_i$ in total, summing over all such nodes yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pi_sum}
  \pi_u + \sum_{w\sim u}\pi_w.
\end{equation}
Also note that, for an edge $(i,j)$ where both endpoints gain degree 1, the change in the product is $(k_i+1)(k_j+1)-k_ik_j = k_i+k_j+1$, but \eqref{eq:pi_sum} counts only $k_i+k_j$. Thus each such edge requires an additional $+1$ correction. The edges with both endpoints in $\{u\}\cup N(u)$ are the $k_u$ edges $(u,w)$ and the $e_u$ edges among neighbors of $u$, so we need to add $k_u+e_u$ to the correction. For the new edges, the new node has degree $k_v=1+k_u$, so their total contribution to $R$ is
\begin{equation}
  (k_u+1)^2 + \sum_{w\sim u}(k_u+1)(k_w+1) = (k_u+1)(2k_u+1+\pi_u). \notag
\end{equation}
Combining all three parts, we get
\begin{equation}
  \Delta R_{\mathrm{all}} = (2+k_u)\pi_u + \sum_{w\sim u}\pi_w + 2k_u^2 + 4k_u + 1 + e_u. \notag
\end{equation}
Thus the expected change in $R$ given target $u$ is $\E[\Delta R\mid u] = (1-p)\,\Delta R_{\mathrm{none}} + p\,\Delta R_{\mathrm{all}}$. Note that 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pi_sum_identity}
  \sum_u\sum_{w\sim u}\pi_w = \sum_w k_w\,\pi_w = 2R,
\end{equation}
as each edge $\{w,z\}$ contributes $k_wk_z$ once on each endpoint. Averaging over the uniform choice of $u$ using~\eqref{eq:pi_sum_identity}, $\sum_u\pi_u = S$, $\sum_u k_u\pi_u = 2R$, $\sum_u e_u = 3T$, $\sum_u k_u = 2M$, and $\sum_u k_u^2 = S$ yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:R_rec}
  R_{N+1} = \left(1\!+\!\frac{4p}{N}\right)R_N + 1 \notag + \frac{(1\!+\!3p)S_N\!+\!(2\!+\!6p)M_N\!+\!3pT_N}{N}. \notag
\end{equation}
Recurrences for both $Q$ and $R$ share the homogeneous growth factor $(1+4p/N)$, with solution $\mathcal{C}_N = \Gamma(N+4p)/[\Gamma(1+4p)\,\Gamma(N)]$. Substituting the exact formulas for $M_N$, $S_N$, $T_N$ from Sections~\ref{sec:edges}--\ref{sec:clustering} into the forcing terms and solving by the same method used for~\eqref{eq:edge_rec},\eqref{eq:tri_rec}, and \eqref{eq:S_rec} yields, for $p\neq 0,\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{1}{4}$,
\begin{align}
  Q_N &= \frac{(26+22p)N}{(1-2p)(1-3p)(1-4p)} - \frac{(12+36p+28p^2)\mathcal{A}_N}{2p^2(1-2p)} \notag \\
  &\quad + \frac{4(3+12p+12p^2)\mathcal{B}_N}{p^2(1-3p)} + \lambda_Q\, \mathcal{C}_N, \notag \\
  R_N &= \frac{3(3+5p)N}{(1-2p)(1-3p)(1-4p)} - \frac{(4+16p+18p^2)\mathcal{A}_N}{2p^2(1-2p)} \notag \\
  &\quad + \frac{2(2+10p+15p^2)\mathcal{B}_N}{p^2(1-3p)} + \lambda_R\, \mathcal{C}_N, \notag
\end{align}
where $\mathcal{A}_N$ and $\mathcal{B}_N$ are as in~\eqref{eq:tri_exact}, and $\lambda_Q$, $\lambda_R$ are constants determined by the initial conditions $Q_1=R_1=0$.

Since $\mathcal{C}_N\sim N^{4p}$, for $p < 1/4$, the homogeneous terms $\mathcal{A}_N$, $\mathcal{B}_N$, $\mathcal{C}_N$ are all sublinear and all five quantities $M,S,T,Q,R$ grow linearly. Let
\[
  \alpha_Q = \frac{26\!+\!22p}{(1\!-\!2p)(1\!-\!3p)(1\!-\!4p)}, \; \alpha_R = \frac{3(3\!+\!5p)}{(1\!-\!2p)(1\!-\!3p)(1\!-\!4p)}.
\]
be the linear term coefficients of $Q_N$ and $R_N$ respectively. From \eqref{eq:edge_rec} and \eqref{eq:S_exact} we can also see that $\alpha_M = 1/(1-2p)$ and $\alpha_S = (6+2p)/[(1-2p)(1-3p)]$. Substituting into \eqref{eq:assortativity_formula} and simplifying, we get that, for $p < 1/4$, as $N \to \infty$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:assort}
  r = \frac{p(9-2p)}{(1+2p)(2-p)} + O(N^{4p - 1}),
\end{equation}
where $O(N^{4p - 1})$ is the correction term derived from $\mathcal{C}_N$.

\section{\label{sec:distance}Average Shortest-Path Distance}

Let $D = \sum_{i<j}d(i,j)$ denote the expected sum of all pairwise shortest-path distances, and write $D_N$ for this sum when the network has $N$ nodes. The average shortest-path distance is then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance_definition}
  \ell = \frac{2D_N}{N(N-1)}.
\end{equation}
At step $N\to N+1$, a target $u$ is chosen uniformly from the $N$ existing nodes. Note that adding $v$ does not shorten any existing pairwise distance, as any path through $v$ can be rerouted through $u$ or its neighbors without increasing length. Thus $D_{N+1} = D_N + \sum_{j}d(v,j)$. Write $\sigma_u = \sum_{j\neq u}d(u,j)$. We now consider the two events separately.

\emph{``None'' event.} The new node $v$ is connected only to the target $u$. Thus, $d(v,u)=1$ and $d(v,j)=1+d(u,j)$ for every $j\neq u$, which yields $\sum_{j}d(v,j) = N + \sigma_u$.

\emph{``All'' event.} The new node $v$ is adjacent to the target $u$ and all its neighbors, so any path from $u$ to $j$ can be rerouted through $v$ by simply replacing the starting point $u$ with $v$. Any path from $v$ to $j$ may also be modified to a path from $u$ to $j$ in the same fashion. Thus, $d(v,j) = d(u,j)$ for all $j \neq u$, and so $\sum_{j}d(v,j) = 1 + \sigma_u$. 

Averaging over the two events and the uniform choice of $u$, and noting that $\frac{1}{N}\sum_u\sigma_u = 2D_N/N$, we have $\sum_{j}d(v,j) = (1 - p)N + p + 2D_N/N$, and so
\begin{equation}\label{eq:D_rec}
  D_{N+1} = \left(1+\frac{2}{N}\right)D_N + (1-p)N + p.
\end{equation}
The homogeneous solution is $\mathcal{D}_N = N(N+1)/2$, satisfying $\mathcal{D}_{N+1}/\mathcal{D}_N = (N+2)/N$. Set $D_N=\gamma_N \mathcal{D}_N$. Then \eqref{eq:D_rec} is reduced to
\begin{equation}
  \gamma_{N+1}-\gamma_N = \frac{2[(1-p)N+p]}{(N+1)(N+2)} = \frac{2(2p-1)}{N+1}+\frac{2(2-3p)}{N+2}. \notag
\end{equation}
It follows from $\gamma_1 = 0$ that
\begin{align} \label{eq:gamma}
  \gamma_N
  &= \sum_{k = 1}^{N - 1} \frac{2(2p-1)}{k+1}+\frac{2(2-3p)}{k+2} \notag \\
  &= 2\!\left( (2p\!-\!1)(H_N\!-\!1) + (2\!-\!3p)(H_{N+1}\!-\!\tfrac{3}{2}) \right),
\end{align}
where $H_N=\sum_{j=1}^{N}1/j$ is the $N$-th harmonic number. Since $H_N\sim\ln N$ as $N \to \infty$, we have $\gamma_N\sim 2(1-p)\ln N$, and so $D_N\sim(1-p)\,N(N+1)\ln N$. Substituting into~\eqref{eq:distance_definition} now yields
\begin{equation}
  \ell \sim 2(1-p)\ln N, \notag
\end{equation}
for all $p\in[0,1)$. At $p=1$ the network is a complete graph with $\ell=1$. The distance scales logarithmically for $p \in (0, 1)$ with no phase transition, confirming the small-world property we established with the constant clustering coefficient from Section~\ref{sec:clustering}.

\section{Consensus Dynamics}

The consensus dynamics of a network is the process of all nodes reaching a common state. Assign each node with a scalar state $x_i \in \R$ and let $L = D - A$ be the Laplacian matrix of the network. The consensus dynamics is defined as $\mathbf{\dot{x}} = -L\mathbf{x}$. Let $0 = \lambda_1 \leq \lambda_2 \leq \cdots \leq \lambda_N$ be the eigenvalues of $L$, and let $v_i$ be the corresponding eigenvectors. Then the consensus dynamics converges to a mean state $\frac{1}{N}\sum_i x_i(0) \mathbf{1}$ at a rate determined by the second smallest eigenvalue $\lambda_2$. Specifically, $\mathbf{x}(t) = \sum_{i} e^{-\lambda_i t} \mathbf{v}_i \mathbf{v}_i^T\mathbf{x}(0)$, and so the contribution of $e^{-\lambda_i t} \mathbf{v}_i \mathbf{v}_i^T\mathbf{x}(0)$ decays exponentially with rate $\lambda_i$ for each $i$. Since $\lambda_2$ is the smallest non-zero eigenvalue, the second term decays the slowest and thus it sets the convergence time $\tau = 1/\lambda_2$. In \cite{DAS2004715}, it is shown that $\lambda_2 \geq 1/(N \cdot \operatorname{diam}(G))$, where $\operatorname{diam}(G)$ is the diameter of the network. Note that the all-or-none network is a random recursive tree at $p = 0$, which has diameter $O(\log N)$ \cite{Pittel1994}. Since the diameter would only decrease as $p$ increases, we have $\lambda_2 = \Omega\left(1/N \log N\right)$. It follows that $\tau = O(N \log N)$. However, this bound is likely not tight, and the \textit{Cheeger inequality} could yield a stronger estimate of $\lambda_2$ if the \textit{conductance} could be bounded directly. We do not pursue this here. On the other hand, the network is a complete graph at $p = 1$, which has Laplacian matrix $L = (N - 1)I - A$. In particular, $A = \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^T - I$, so $L = N \cdot I - \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^T$, which has eigenvalue $0$ with multiplicity $1$ and eigenvalue $N$ with multiplicity $N - 1$. Thus $\lambda_2 = N$ and the timescale for consensus is $\tau = 1/N$ at $p = 1$. This implies that $\tau$ collapses from $O(N \log N)$ to $1/N$ as $p$ increases from $0$ to $1$, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:timescale}.

\section{\label{sec:conclusion}Conclusion}

We have presented an analytical study of the all-or-none copying model, showing that its single parameter $p$ governs a rich variety of network phenomena. The edge count exhibits a sparse-to-dense transition at $p=1/2$, while the degree distribution follows a power law $P_k \sim k^{-1/p}$, recovering the exponent in the BA model at $p=1/3$ without invoking preferential attachment. A clustering coefficient $C=3p/(1+2p)$ independent of $N$ and logarithmic distances $\ell \sim 2(1-p)\ln N$ establish the small-world property for every $p \in (0,1)$, and a positive assortativity $r = p(9-2p)/[(1+2p)(2-p)]$ for $p<1/4$. For the network dynamics, our logarithmic diameter bound yields a consensus timescale of $\tau = O(N\log N)$ for general $p$, while the complete-graph limit at $p=1$ gives $\tau = 1/N$, confirming that a minimal copying rule can produce realistic network structure and efficient dynamics simultaneously.
